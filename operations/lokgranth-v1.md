## 1. Internal Team Operations

The Internal Team Ops document holds our practice. It captures the day-to-day of how projects move, how roles function, how decisions are made, and how we stay in sync as individuals and as a team. One that helps us navigate our work with clarity, care, and collaboration. It is for the Lokwasis, by the Lokwasis.

---

### Our Philosophy of Collaboration

> "Collaboration allows us to know more than we are capable of knowing by ourselves." — Paul Solarz

At Devalok, feedback sharpens thinking, dialogue deepens empathy, and shared responsibility creates better outcomes. No role is small, and no idea is above critique. Good work becomes great when seen, questioned, shaped, and refined by multiple perspectives. This way, collaboration is not just a step in our process — It is the process.

#### Design is not an isolated act

Design grows stronger when it passes through multiple hands, minds, and perspectives. Isolation may lead to speed, but collaboration leads to depth. We design for people, and that means designing with people, too. The number one rule of our collaboration philosophy is: **No one works in a silo. Ever.**

**Share early, share ugly**
Don't wait for things to be polished. Sharing work-in-progress opens space for new perspectives and helps spot problems before they harden.
- Share rough sketches and visual directions early.
- Start critique sessions with WIPs, not just finals.

**Co-create when stuck**
If you're blocked, don't stay stuck. Jump on a co-work call, schedule a 15-min whiteboard, or ask someone to think it through with you. Fresh brains unlock momentum. "Can I borrow your brain for 10 minutes?" = a culture worth waiting for.

**Seek feedback with intention**
Be specific about what you need. It helps others give better input and prevents vague or overwhelming responses.
- "Thoughts?" or "What do you think?"
- "Does this layout work according to the client feedback?" or "I'm unsure about the colours I used here, can you take a look?"

**Document your thought processes**
Add notes, voice recordings, or even quick messages to share what's going on in your head. If someone isn't available for a call, don't wait — send them something they can catch up on.
- Figma notes, comments, Google Docs, Google Chat messages, voice notes all work.
- This keeps collaboration async-friendly, especially across time zones or schedules.

**Acknowledge wins!**
Design is never a solo win. If someone's feedback, words, or even a passing comment shaped your thinking, acknowledge it.
- "That visual idea came from Mudit's sketch!"
- "This version is way better thanks to Chhavi's feedback."

It costs nothing. It builds everything.

**Talk in the open**
If a project involves more than two people, don't keep the conversation in DMs.
- Use group chats or allocated channels to share updates, ask questions, and keep everyone in the loop.
- Keep the thread going with status updates, progress checks, clarifications, and even silly jokes.

The work becomes better when the room is close.

#### Roles ≠ Hierarchy

We believe in responsibility, not rank. Our titles are defined as roles of responsibility rather than superiority because we don't function like a corporate ladder. A DA might lead one project and assist on another. An ADA could lead a project while assisting on a different one, with an Apprentice taking the lead.

| Role | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Apprentice** | In training. Learns by doing. The focus is on skill-building and exposure. |
| **ADA (Asst. Design Associate)** | Understands the right process. Works independently and is focused on cross-disciplinary experience. |
| **DA (Design Associate)** | Expert in at least one area. Has cross-disciplinary awareness. Mentors ADAs and fellow DAs. |
| **DD (Design Director)** | Provides creative direction. Supports multiple teams. Elevates quality and consistency. |
| **Founder** | Sets direction, steps in when needed, supports overall project health. |

**Responsibility is what matters** — If someone's leading a project, they're in charge of that flow, even if they're "junior" in another context.

**Lead by example, not by authority** — If you're leading a project, take the initiative, check in, and listen more than you speak.

**Leadership is rotational** — You may lead one project and assist in another. Stay fluid and humble.

#### Seeking & Giving Feedback: The 2A3B

**Seeking Feedback: Give direction to get direction**

- Don't be vague, be intentional. When asking for feedback, be clear about what you're looking for: validation, fresh eyes, specific direction, or inspiration?
- Don't just drop a screenshot and say, "thoughts?" Instead, share the context — what were you trying to solve? What influenced this direction? What kind of input do you need, and from whom?
- Good feedback starts with good communication. Lay the ground so the team understands where you're coming from and how to help you move forward.

**The feedback cycle:** Explain → Listen → Question → Implement → Next Steps

**Giving Feedback: Build a culture of care**

Critique to us is about pointing forward. We speak with kindness to help someone (and something) grow:

| Principle | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Ask Questions** | Question the work's source, thought process and absorb their thinking before critiquing it. |
| **Acknowledge** | Point out things that work and why. Critique is not just about problems — also acknowledging good work. |
| **Be Timely** | Give feedback when it can still shape the outcome. Be mindful of timelines. |
| **Be Specific** | Always explain why something works or doesn't. Every little detail matters to shape a new direction. |
| **Be the Guide** | Help them understand how to approach the idea better and push them in the right direction. |

**Build confidence with feedback**

Some responses are vague and can feel personal. They don't offer direction, and the person on the other end might feel discouraged. That's not how we do things at Devalok. Feedback should build confidence, not break it.

**Shutdown phrases (never use):**
- "I don't like it."
- "This feels off."
- "This doesn't work."
- "This isn't what I expected."
- "You're not getting it right."

**Constructive phrases (use instead):**
- "You're onto something. What if we tweak just this one piece?"
- "Let's revisit this — right now, it's not landing as intended."
- "What was the intention here? It may have come across differently than expected."
- "This might not work for this project, but let's explore why."

### How We Work as Individuals & as a Team

> "A team is not a group of people who work together. A team is a group of people who trust each other." — Simon Sinek

At Devalok, we believe that the team is only as strong as its individuals, and individuals are only as effective as the support and trust they're given. We honour each person's unique working style, strengths, and limits. Flexibility, clarity, and self-awareness are essential. We design better when we know how to work with ourselves and each other.

#### Flexibility in work styles

Everyone works differently — and that's not something we try to "fix." Some people start early, and others work better at night. Some like structure, others thrive in flow. At Devalok, we honour individual rhythms. What matters is communication, not control. Flexibility is not a lack of structure, it's trust in motion.

**Update your status**
Show your availability using Karm, Google Calendar, and chat status. This helps the team plan and stay in sync.
- Apply for leaves according to The Break Framework
- Put an OOO on your calendar for leave days
- Update your Google Chat status if you're AFK

**Communicate your schedule**
Are you starting late? Taking a break? Working from somewhere new? Let the team know so they can plan around it. Dropping in a "I will be available only after 2 PM today" message on the group is more than enough.

**Understand your teammates' work flow**
Know who likes morning deep work vs. evening hours. This helps with timelines and expectations.
- Talk to them or check their chat status.
- Set internal deadlines according to your schedules.

**Own your method of tracking**
Whether it's Karm, docs, notes, or your own system — we don't enforce one system for tracking daily work. There is no one tracking your daily work but yourself. You are your own manager.

**Self-regulate your workload**
If you're overwhelmed, say so. If you have bandwidth, offer help. Learn to say no, yes, and "not yet." This will help you more than anyone and also build your self-confidence.

**Respect everyone's quiet zones and focus hours**
If someone's marked themselves unavailable or busy, don't expect instant responses. Let people find their beat.

**Make room for slow days and bursts of energy**
Every day won't look the same. Honour your own cycle and respect that others have one too. Set deadlines for low-priority projects according to the collective creative health of the team.

**Celebrate different ways of thinking**
Not everyone thinks the same — some are visual, and some are verbal. Let that shape how we work.

### Project Rhythm

> "Structure is not the enemy of creativity. It's the scaffolding that holds it up." — Austin Kleon

Good work needs a rhythm of momentum, clarity, and accountability. Projects thrive when everyone knows who's doing what, the goal, and how progress moves forward. We don't believe in micromanaging but don't leave things floating. The balance lies in creating just enough structure to keep everyone aligned without suffocating creativity.

#### Managing project allocations

Project allocation is how we set the foundation for any project by deciding who leads, supports, and joins. At Devalok, project assignments are thoughtful and based on each person's strengths, aspirations, and bandwidth. This allows the team to grow, stay balanced, and deliver excellent work without burnout.

Project allocation begins with the Founder, Mudit, who holds a clear view of the studio's ongoing and upcoming projects. When a new project comes in, Mudit assigns project leads and members based on two key factors:

**Individual strengths & goals** — Through 1:1s with Mudit, each team member shares their aspirations, skills, and growth areas. These conversations ensure that allocation isn't just based on past experience but also on what someone wants to explore and improve.

**Bandwidth & availability** — Everyone is expected to update their availability with Mudit. This helps ensure project teams are formed fairly.

*[Note: Bandwidth visibility is now tracked through Karm's built-in bandwidth feature, replacing the manual format described in Lokgranth v1.]*

#### Workload limits

- **No one leads more than two active projects at a time.** Leading a project requires presence, ownership, and decision-making. We don't stretch anyone too thin to protect creative energy and ensure quality. If you're currently leading two projects, you won't be assigned to lead another until it's completed or moved into a passive phase.

- **No one co-leads more than two active projects.** Co-leading still takes time — check-ins, reviews, creative contributions. If you feel able to take on more, it should be discussed with the team. The point is to keep workloads balanced.

#### Leading a project

A project lead is responsible for direction, delivery, momentum, and team alignment. They guide client communications, define the project flow, and ensure everyone has what they need to contribute meaningfully. They're not the boss but hold the direction and keep the project moving.

**Initial Phase:**
- Lead the creation of a custom brand workshop experience with your co-lead.
- Decide who from the team joins at which stages of the project.
- Create a realistic timeline of the project and plan on how to stick to it as best as possible.

**Project Kick Off:**
- Set up and update the project record (Karyakram) with all key info — contacts, links, files, and feedback logs.
- Assign a record-keeper based on team bandwidth. This role includes updating Karyakram and adding meeting notes, decisions, and key links.
- You have the authority to define how the team tracks timelines and updates. This can vary by project — one team might do weekly check-ins every Wednesday, another might do alternate-day check-ins.
- Keep project momentum alive by posting updates, reminders, or check-ins in the group chat.

**Managing Delays & Accountability:**
- If a critical task is delayed, the project lead follows up. Clients don't see individual roles — they see Devalok. Ownership lies with the lead.
- Follow up and unblock your team if there is a feedback delay beyond 24 hours.
- Always make sure you keep the team and the Client POCs updated on shifts in the timeline due to unforeseen delays or feedback iterations.

**Client Communication:**
- Everything shared with the client — drafts, workshops, decks — should reflect high-quality work, even in early stages.
- While team members may interact with the client based on task assignments, the project lead ensures:
  - No client emails, messages or requests go unanswered for more than 24 hours.
  - Replies are prompt, clear, and complete.
  - In case of conflicts, consult the Founder and the team for quick and proper resolution.

### Project Beginnings

> "The key is to set realistic client expectations and then exceed them — preferably in unexpected and helpful ways." — Richard Branson

The beginning of every project sets the tone for everything that follows. This section establishes the phases, including the first client call, sharing our philosophy and process, and the following collaborative onboarding. It's where expectations are aligned, responsibilities are defined, and the real partnership begins.

#### Initial Client Interaction

This is where the relationship begins. The first call sets the tone for collaboration — it's a space to talk openly, understand each other, and see if we're the right creative partners.

**The First Call**
Mudit holds the first call with the client. He walks them through a deck that introduces our philosophy, past work, and why branding and design matter. This is **Deck 01**. It will be adapted based on the client's context.

Objective of the first call:
- Understand the client's business, goals, and needs
- Share Devalok's approach and philosophy
- Check for creative and cultural alignment (vibe check)
- Pick clients' minds for non-negotiable additions to the brand/work

**Post First Call**
If both sides feel aligned, Mudit shares:
- The same deck shown in the call
- A custom proposal document with investment details and a rough timeline
- It is established that the final timeline will be confirmed after the onboarding call
- The client confirms the partnership

**Onboarding Preparation**
Once the partnership is confirmed, we prepare the ground for deep, collaborative work:
- Mudit assigns a project lead and co-lead based on individual strengths, aspirations, and bandwidth
- He briefs them on the client background, goals, and expectations
- The project lead and co-lead, with support from Mudit, prepare a custom brand workshop and an onboarding questionnaire deck — **Deck 02**
- The deck is shared with the clients to prepare for the onboarding call

**Onboarding Call & Brand Workshop**
- The lead introduces the team and their roles
- The client introduces their team and context (this builds mutual understanding)
- The lead explains the workshop flow and invites collaboration
- The workshop is conducted using a custom FigJam board
- The call ends with clearly stated next steps, which include sending the clients a phase-wise project deck with timelines — **Deck 03**
- Setting up of the project team and work begins immediately

### Karyakram

> "The faintest ink is more powerful than the strongest memory." — Chinese Proverb

Every project needs a centre — where everything lives and nothing gets lost. Karyakram is our project recordkeeping system, designed to be a single source of truth. It brings together scattered updates, links, notes, and goals into one accessible space. It helps teams stay aligned, transitions stay smooth, and work stays clear, no matter who joins when.

*[Note: Karyakram was originally maintained as Google Docs with a shared template. It has since been rebuilt as a full task management system within Karm — with stages, kanban boards, tasks, deliverables, threaded messages, and file attachments. The principles below still apply to how project records should be maintained.]*

#### Why it matters

- It consolidates all scattered information across emails, portal updates, and Google Chat messages into one accessible place.
- It keeps a log of all project calls with recordings and meeting notes.
- It stores critical project links: Figma files, proposal decks, feedback documents, and brand asset folders.
- It makes onboarding new team members easier — no one has to chase links or ask for background.
- It enables long-term access to project assets for retainer clients, ensuring we can support them even months down the line.
- It provides clarity during moments of confusion — everyone knows where to check first before asking.
- It helps uphold accountability across the team — when it's logged, it's real.

#### Who keeps the record, and when

- The project lead assigns the record-keeper. Depending on availability and capacity, this can be themselves or someone on the team.
- Assignment happens right after the onboarding call, so Karyakram can be set up while the project context is still fresh.
- If a record-keeper is not explicitly assigned, the responsibility stays with the project lead.
- The record-keeper updates Karyakram regularly — after every client call, internal check-in, or major decision. This includes notes, links, updated timelines, and feedback logs.
- The role can rotate if needed, especially for longer projects. Clear communication is key if responsibilities shift.
- If a project moves into retainer mode, someone should still be assigned to maintain the doc, even at a lower frequency, so that history and handovers stay intact.

#### Getting started

- Start by setting up the project in Karyakram and filling in the basics.
- Add the client's basic info and clearly defined goals and objectives from the onboarding call.
- As the project unfolds, the assigned record-keeper should update the record regularly — with meeting logs, client feedback, revised timelines, and updated links.
