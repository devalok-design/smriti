## Overview

This document defines how Devalok creates LinkedIn content for **Mudit Lal's personal account**. The goal is thought leadership that serves the studio's ICP (Ideal Client Profile) while maintaining Mudit's authentic voice.

LinkedIn content is NOT studio marketing. It's perspective-driven writing that demonstrates expertise through specific, researched, grounded insight.

## Voice Rules (LinkedIn-Specific)

These are in addition to the general Devalok voice rules. When writing LinkedIn posts, follow ALL of these:

### Hard Rules

| Rule | Rationale |
|------|-----------|
| **No em dashes (—)** | Mudit's voice doesn't use them. Use commas, periods, or restructure. |
| **No "it's not X, it's Y" structures** | Overused LinkedIn pattern. Feels cliched. |
| **No motivational closers** | No "go build something great" or "your brand deserves better." End with substance. |
| **No buzzwords** | Never: thought leader, game-changer, transformative, innovative, disruptive, unlock potential, next level. |
| **No vague fillers** | Never: "in today's world", "the truth is", "here's the thing", "let that sink in." |
| **"We" for Devalok work** | When referencing studio observations, projects, or philosophy. |
| **"I" only for personal** | Only for genuinely personal experiences, not studio work reframed as personal. |
| **No word limits** | Content drives length. A post is as long as it needs to be. |
| **Mandatory research** | Every post must include real companies, real numbers, specific backstories. No hypotheticals. |
| **Clear takeaway** | Every post ends with something concrete and practical the reader can use. |

### Quality Standard

| Dimension | How It Should Sound |
|-----------|---------------------|
| Perspective | A founder sharing what they see from doing the work |
| Tone | Calm authority, not performance |
| Grounding | Specific, researched, with real examples |
| Identity | Builder-first, not influencer |

## Content Architecture

### Three Personas (ICP Targeting)

| Persona | Description | Content Focus |
|---------|-------------|---------------|
| **SMBs in the US** | Small-medium businesses, often founder-led, pre-Series A or bootstrapped | How design creates competitive advantage at smaller scale |
| **VC Firms** | Venture capital, evaluating brand as part of investment thesis | Brand as signal of founder quality and market positioning |
| **Enterprises** | Larger companies considering rebrand or design system investment | Design ROI, brand consistency at scale, ecosystem thinking |

### Four Content Pillars

| Pillar | Share | Description |
|--------|-------|-------------|
| **Thought Leadership** | 30% | Industry observations, design philosophy, contrarian takes backed by evidence |
| **Social Proof** | 30% | Case studies, results, client stories (always with permission) |
| **Personal Growth** | 10% | Founder journey, lessons learned, behind-the-scenes |
| **Tips & Tricks** | 30% | Practical, actionable advice the ICP can use immediately |

## Post Structure

Every LinkedIn post follows this arc:

```
Hook          → Surprising fact, question, or counterintuitive opener
Context/Story → Real company, real data, real backstory
Insight       → What this means — the deeper pattern
Principle     → The transferable lesson
Close         → Concrete takeaway or framework the reader can apply
```

### Structural Rules

- **Hook**: Must stop the scroll. First line is everything. No generic openers.
- **Context**: Real story, not hypothetical. Include specifics (company name, year, numbers).
- **Insight**: Draw out the non-obvious pattern. This is where the value lives.
- **Close**: Practical. Not motivational. Something the reader can DO.
- **Whitespace**: Use line breaks liberally. Dense paragraphs die on LinkedIn.

## Research Requirements

Every post MUST include real data. No hypotheticals, no "imagine a company that..."

### What Counts as Research

- **Company backstory**: founding year, key pivot moments, growth trajectory
- **Specific numbers**: revenue, valuation, growth percentages, spend comparisons
- **Named people**: founders, designers, decision-makers who drove the change
- **Time context**: when things happened, how long they took

### Research Sources

- Company annual reports, SEC filings, press releases
- Industry publications (Packaging of the World, Brand New, It's Nice That)
- Interviews and podcasts with founders/designers
- Market analysis from credible sources
- Devalok's own case studies (from Manas)

### Example of Good Research

> "Oatly was founded in 1985 as a niche health food brand. For nearly 20 years, they stayed small. Then in 2012, new CEO Toni Petersson brought in Forsman & Bodenfors. The rebrand didn't just change packaging. It changed the way people talked about oat milk. Demand surged 700%. They went from a Swedish health food footnote to a $10B IPO in 2021."

## Example Post with Analysis

### The Post (Draft v4)

**Target Persona:** SMBs in the US
**Content Pillar:** Thought Leadership (30%)
**Word Count:** ~390 words

```
Oatly was a health food brand for 20 years before anyone noticed.

Founded in 1985 by a food scientist at Lund University, they sold oat-based drinks to a niche audience of lactose-intolerant consumers. For nearly two decades, they stayed in that lane.

Then in 2012, new CEO Toni Petersson brought in Forsman & Bodenfors. They didn't just redesign the packaging. They redesigned how people felt about oat milk.

The quirky hand-lettered cartons. The "Wow, No Cow" campaign. The deliberately unpolished tone that made a health product feel like a cultural statement.

Demand surged 700%. Oatly grew at a 21% compound annual rate. By 2021, they went public at a $10 billion valuation.

That's what design does for a smaller brand. It closes the gap that budget can't.

Consider the math. Allbirds spent roughly $400,000 to $500,000 per month on advertising when they launched. Nike was spending $3.59 billion a year. That's roughly $300 million every month. The difference is over 600x.

Allbirds couldn't outspend Nike. So they out-designed them. A simple wool shoe, a clean color palette, a brand voice that felt like a friend, not a corporation. Four years later, Allbirds hit a $1.7 billion valuation.

Three things happen when design does this well:

1. The brand stops needing to explain itself. The visual identity does the talking. Oatly's packaging sits on a shelf next to Silk and Califia. You know which one it is without reading the label.

2. Perception does the selling. Allbirds wasn't selling shoes. They were selling a feeling. That feeling came from design, not from features.

3. You earn the right to charge more. When your brand looks considered, people assume your product is considered. Premium perception follows premium design.

For smaller brands, this isn't a luxury. It's the strategy.

Three questions we ask every brand we work with:
What do you actually stand for?
Can someone understand it in 3 seconds by looking at your presence?
Does every surface of your brand say the same thing?

If the answer to any of these is no, design isn't a nice-to-have. It's the unlock.
```
